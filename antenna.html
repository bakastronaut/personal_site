Problem

The advent of the CubeSat provided lower-cost access to space, opening the domain to academic institutions, small businesses, and developing states. However, this benefit came at the cost of reduced performance relative to monolithic, traditional satellites. This is particularly true for radio frequency sensors requiring large antenna apertures such as high-bandwidth communications, remote sensing, and synthetic aperture radar imaging. Their use with the volume-limited nature of CubeSats demands large aperture-to-stowed-volume ratios. Overcoming these limitations can bring a host of new sensors to CubeSats, augmenting their capability and enabling new architectures.

Approach

Rigid deployable antennas have been explored as a solution. Howeverk they suffer from small aperture-to-stowed-volume ratios, requiring smaller apertures (reduced gain). Unfortunately, improvements are impeded by fundamental limitations in material strength-to-volume ratios. Inflatable antennas' utilization of thin films solves the issue facing rigid deployables. However, these films are not rigid and cause reduced surface precision (reduced gain). We chose to tackle this problem from an inflatables standpoint due to limited exploration in literature and areas of potential improvement regarding surface precision and alignment.

The traditional space hardware design approach is to focus on precision engineering and fabrication on the front-end for well-aligned flight assemblies. The same approach has been used in previous inflatables research. However, it is generally a lost cause due to the low-precision and low-accuracy nature of thin film inflatables. To put this in perspective, an RMS reflector surface error as small as 3 mm reduces 16 GHz gain by 10 dBi. Our approach is to use back-of-the-envelope design with strategic assembly to maximize post-assembly adjustability. More specifically, this method emphasizes adaptive assembly processes to handle fabrication imperfections and design modularity to increase its degrees of freedom.

Results

Our design has four key contributions to inflatable antenna development:

  1. Inflatable antenna with 2.4 meter diameter primary with empirical measurements exceeding state of the art X-band gain by over 13 dBi
  1. Inflatable antenna with empirical primary reflector surface efficiency improvement exceeding 6 dB and a 240% increase in primary reflector aperture diameter over state of the art
  1. A novel three-chamber modular design enabling on-orbit alignment and wrinkle removal
  1. Flexible design approach emphasizing post-assembly design adjustability rather than a design precision focus
