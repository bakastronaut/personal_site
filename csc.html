Caltech Space Challenge

Problem

The Caltech Space Challenge is an intense, one-week mission design competition for students. Thirty-two undergraduate and graduate students with a variety of backgrounds and nationalities are split into two teams of sixteen. The two teams are given a challenge and pitted against each other, resulting in a grueling, sleepless, caffeine-filled challenge to develop a human space mission. At the end of the week, each team submits a proposal and presents its results to a panel of representatives from the aerospace industry.

In merely five days, the teams are challenged to design a human mission to land on a Martian moon, either Deimos or Phobos, and return them safely of Earth along with a sample. The missionâ€™s launch date must be no later than January 1st, 2041.

Approach

My role involved the coordination of the round-trip spacecraft trajectory design, as well as simulations regarding communication blackouts, surface mapping strategies, and negotiating trajectory-propulsion tradeoffs with the propulsion team. I worked closely with my trajectory design partner Karin Schlottke to derive a preliminary trajectory design. This design was supplied to the propulsion team and human factors teams to provide an initial framework. Once the hand-calculated trajectory was refined and modified to mesh with the mission architecture, I began designing the geocentric, heliocentric, and areocentric trajectories using Systems Tool Kit (STK). Once the detailed trajectory was modeled, I played a key role in system architecture tradeoff analysis involving the feasibility and utility of areocentric operation alternatives.

Results

At the conclusion of the competition, each team composed a formal proposal and presented its mission architecture to a panel of judges. The panel was composed of Buzz Aldrin and senior members of NASA, JPL, Caltech, Lockheed Martin, and SpaceX. The judges announced my team,Team Voyager, as the winner.

The resulting trajectory was a round-trip, 465 day, opposition class mission with a launch date in April 2033, returning in July 2034. Once the spacecraft achieves a Mars orbit insertion, it will orbit Mars for 30 days while the crew performs operations on the surface of Phobos. To reach Phobos, the spacecraft was stationed in a parking orbit until the spacecraft and Phobos had the correct phase difference. The transfer was composed of a bi-elliptic, 2-plane Hohmann transfer with an apse rotation of 8 degrees and a periapsis raise of 9000 km. The spacecraft then arrived in a circular, Phobos-trailing orbit. The humans then entered the Surface Exploration Vehicle and detached from the mothership to perform Phobos surface operations.

Impact

Following the competition, Team Voyager published its proposal in the 2014 Journal of Space Operations & Communicator. Our findings have been cited in literature, and used by subsequent Caltech Space Challenges as well as other space architecture design competitions around the world.
