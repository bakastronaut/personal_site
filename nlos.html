NLOS Polarization

Problem

Non-line-of-sight (NLOS) imaging involves imaging items occluded by opaque or transluscent objects such as walls or biological tissue. NLOS imaging is typically performed using active sensors such as radars or lasers, enabling operation in non-ideal environments. However, passive NLOS imaging use has been used sparingly due to ill-posed light transport. Improvements in passive NLOS imaging can benefit search & rescue, remote sensing, and stealth operations.

Approach

The approach to improve passive NLOS imaging is to leverage a dimension of light commonly excluded and underutilized: polarization. Polarization is useful because it contains information about the orientation and roughness of each surface it interacted with while traveling from the light source to the camera. Light transport models, or shaders, that consider polarization commonly assume the light interacts with perfectly smooth surfaces. However, in reality, most surfaces are rough (diffuse). Shaders for unpolarized light and diffuse surfaces do exist and are used widely in 3D computer graphics (Lambert, Oren-Nayar). For this project, the Cook-Torrance shader (quasi-diffuse) is combined with the Stokes-Mueller polarization formulation, resulting in a computationally efficient diffuse polarization shader.

The Cook-Torrance shader assumes the intensity measured by a camera pixel to be a combined result of photons from ambient lighting and photons from a number of light sources within the scene. So for a single camera pixel, there are N rays traced through the scene, with a final addition step for ambient illumination, resulting in N+1 operations (assuming only 1 bounce for each ray). This says that if I know what's on the other side of the wall x and use that as an input to my physics model A, I can determine the image y my camera will acquire. The NLOS imaging problem is the inverse: Given a captured image y, and the physics model A, estimate the unknown input x such that the difference between my photograph and the camera image predicted by physics is minimized (subject to some constraints). This inverted process is much more difficult since it requires undoing the scrambling of light (entropy) introduced by the rough surface. Due to the nature of constraints, this optimization problem is solved via Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers.
