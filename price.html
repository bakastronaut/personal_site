Problem

Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment Follow-On (GRACE-FO) is a NASA space research mission composed of two satellites with the objective to measure and map the Earth's gravity field. Localized changes in gravity, such as those caused by water & ice migration, change the force on a spacecraft and causes it to speed-up or slow-down. One trails the other in orbit, so the change in their separation caused by gravity can be measured to estimate the gravitational force.

GRACE-FO is a follow-on to GRACE, flying a new laser-ranging interferometer (LRI) as a technology demonstration for enhanced inter-satellite range measurements. The technical challenge is integrating the LRI with minimal added risk and is the main responsibility of payload accommodation systems engineers. I was hired as a payload accommodation systems engineering intern to help quantify, manage, and track risk introduced by the LRI.

Risk is tracked and assessed via Level 3 (L3) flight system requirements, which cover quantitative performance requirements for the spacecraft (e.g. Newtons of thrust required for orbit control). The Spacecraft Design Spec and General Design Interface Requirements are derived from L3's, which have complex inter-dependencies, and must be flown-up to L3, L2, and L1 requirements to ensure consistency. During this process, an instrument's design change necessitated lower operating temperatures. I was tasked with assessing the impact of this change.

Approach

This contractor captures the the spacecraft-/intstrument-level design hierarchy in two documents: the Spacecraft Design Specification (SDS), and the General Design Interface Requirements (GDIR). JPL sees these two as a single document, the Interface Control Document (ICD), wherein all the requirements for system-instrument and instrument-intstrument interfaces are detailed. Prior to performing the impact assessment, I needed to convert the two external documents to an internal ICD.

The SDS & GDIR contain hundreds of complex inter-dependencies, so merging must be completed without losing or violating these links. TO merge without losing these links, I developed a recursive searching & linking algorithm to derive the document with conflicts flagged.

System design is the process of achieving a single mission goal amidst many competing interests. As a result, the designer must be judiciously flexible. There were four potential courses of action:

  1. Redesign instrument to withstand higher temperatures
  2. Redesign spacecraft thermal system to provide lower temperatures
  3. Issue requirement violation waiver
  4. Submit requirement change request
I identified three importnat trade-offs:
  1. Risk
  2. Resources (time, cost, key personnel, facilities)
  3. Utility

Results

My impact assessment of the requested operational temperature changes required running thermal models to estimate new in-flight temperature ranges and determined the changed load on the thermal system. My findings were:

  • No added risk to the thermal system to maintain lower temperatures (results in 8 Watts of power savings).
  • Minimal risk added to isntrument -- 30 Celsius of temperature margin is retained over nominal operation levels, requiring no changes in environmental test specification.
I consulted engineers & stakeholders, received a statement of concurrence, and presented the negotiated change request to a project change board. The change request was approved, officiating adoption for new power/thermal preliminary design review.

Impact

The impact of my contributions led to successful system design, development, and flight. GRACE-FO was launched via a Falcon 9 on May 22, 2018 and continues to generate sub-monthly environmental data vital to tracking climate change and fresh water supplies.
