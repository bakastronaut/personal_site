Caltech Space Challenge

Problem

Optics in the City of Light is a competitive summer research experience for undergraduates that funds students to perform research in Paris, France. I was fortunate to spend my summer at the oldest institution of optics research and higher education, Institut d'optique, which specializes in the development of nanophotonics, lasers, optical systems, and quantum optics technologies. Institut d'optique previously delivered critical optics for the NASA STEREO mission which studied solar activity in the ultraviolet regime. Follow-on work to study deeper layers of the Sun require imaging X-rays. Traditional mirrors are not suitable since X-rays are absorbed by standard materials due to excitation of atoms' k-band electrons. My research involved the theoretical & experimental design, testing, and characterization of multilayer mirrors for the reflection of soft X-rays.

Approach

For theoretical design, I used thin film interference theory to determine the optimal layer thickness for a three-layer (2 boundaries) multilayer structure. This requires knowing the phase of each reflected and transmitted wave at each boundary. The phase shift is not easily determined because as a wave travels through a medium, it loses energy, causing a path-length-dependent phase shift. The problem is solved when the layer thicknesses collectively produce constructive interference at the top of the stack. A 3-layer mirror produces low reflectivity. However, if materials are chosen carefully, the reflectivity will increase as the number of layers increases. Therefore, after solving the 2-boundary problem, I chose Amorphous Carbon and Cobalt as materials and used IMD (commercial optics software) to determine the reflectivity of mirrors with roughly 200 layers. I then began optimizing three periodic designs.

After 50 design iterations, I arrived at an optimized design. Thereafter, I utilized the Magnetron Sputtering technique to create 2 versions of one of my designs in a Class 1000 (ISO 6) cleanroom. Both designs consisted of alternating layers of amorphous Carbon and Cobalt. One version utilized a float glass substrate which has great mechanical properties as the substrate but allows some light to exit the stack. The other had a silicon substrate which is opaque and helps contain light, but it is less stiff and has a larger suface height deviation. After deposition, each mirror was characterized using X-ray diffractometry.

Results

Results showed that the float glass substrate mirror produced a Bragg's peak reflectivity value of 0.01%, while the silicon substrate mirror displayed no Bragg's peak. Performance was drastically different from experimental designs, which predicted reflectivities of 61%. It turned-out that "small" defects play a large role in the mirror's performance. When each layer is only 1.5 nm (a few atoms) thick, interfacial diffusion, layer thickness errors, and surface roughness drastically change the mirror's geometry. Reflectivity curve fitting suggested the presence of high layer roughness values averaging 1.09 nm and layer thickness errors. In order to achieve maximum reflectivity, the deposition process must be optimized to minimize roughness and layer thickness errors.
